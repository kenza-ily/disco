In healthcare, what a patient says is rarely the whole story, and that's exactly where artificial
intelligence still falls short. Unlike consumer tech, where a request is often literal, clinical
interactions are layered with fear, context, and unspoken concerns. When Al takes what's said at
face value, it risks misunderstanding the nature of care.
That's the warning from Tamara Saver, who highlights how large language models (LLMs) often miss
the "why behind the why." A teenager asking for a CT scan, or a woman seeking a back x-ray at 3
a.m., aren't just making medical requests; they're revealing more profound anxieties. A good doctor
recognizes this and adjusts accordingly. Al, as it stands today, doesn't.
This chapter explores where LLMs can deliver value in healthcare and where they can't. When the
question is straightforward and the data structured, AI can retrieve records, summarize instructions,
and surface insights with impressive speed.
But when patient needs are emotional, complex, or evolving, Al falters. It can't interpret ambiguity. It
can't empathize. And it certainly can't build the mental model a physician constructs instinctively.
Yet Saver is no AI skeptic. Her work at Vituity shows how the correct implementation makes all the
difference. A sepsis prediction model embedded invisibly into lab panels led to earlier diagnoses
and saved lives.
A reframed tool, pitched as an "Al Resident" rather than an all-knowing system, earned clinician
trust and improved adoption. These examples prove successful healthcare Al isn't just about more
innovative models. It's about seamless design, thoughtful deployment, and earning trust by acting
as an assistant, not a replacement.
The future of Al in healthcare doesn't depend on hype or abstraction; it depends on precision,
humility, and understanding where these tools truly fit. When AI supports genuine human care, not
just digital efficiency, it can make medicine faster and more humane.

The problem: AI in healthcare needs clear intent
A lot of AI failures in healthcare happen because systems misread the intent behind what
patients or clinicians say. Large language models often take requests at face value, but any
doctor will tell you: the hard part is figuring out what's really going on beneath the surface.
Tamara Saver shares two examples that bring this to life:
In one, a 16-year-old shows up in the ER asking for a CT scan. His parents
had plugged his symptoms into ChatGPT and were worried it was
appendicitis. But after talking with the patient, the doctor realizes it's not
about sharp pain: it's months of ongoing nausea. Totally different issue,
totally different path forward. The family was chasing one answer, but
the clinical reality pointed somewhere else.
In another, a woman comes in at 3 a.m. asking for a back x-ray.
She's had pain for years. So why now? Turns out her brother was just
diagnosed with cancer, and she's scared. She's not just looking for a
scan, she's looking for reassurance.
These stories highlight what AI can miss: the deeper meaning behind a question or request.
Good care depends on understanding a person's full story, history, fears, and context, not just
what they say out loud. Tamara Saver puts it well: skilled clinicians are trained to spot fear,
doubt, and what's left unsaid. LLMs, for now, just aren't built to pick up on that kind of emotional
and situational depth.

Where LLMs work and where they fail in healthcare
Where LLMs excel
Saver points out a few places where large language models really can add
value in clinical settings:
Searching structured medical data
When the question is straightforward and tied to existing records, LLMs can
be a big help. They can quickly pull up a patient's cardiac history, current
medications, or past imaging reports. Thanks to semantic search, doctors
can spot patterns and find results that would usually take time and manual
effort to dig through.
Creating patient-friendly communication
AI can help draft discharge notes in plain language that patients actually
understand. Take a dancer with a dislocated shoulder, for example; rather
than generic rehab advice, AI can tailor the message to encourage recovery
in a way that aligns with dance goals, making it more personal
and motivating.
Answering factual questions
Patient portals get many simple queries like, "When's my next tetanus shot?"
These don't require deep thinking, just quick and accurate answers. LLMs can
handle that well, pulling from existing records with speed and precision.
X
Where LLMs fail
But there are serious limits to what they can do:
Understanding deeper intent
LLMs can't read between the lines or offer empathy. If someone asks, "Do I
have cancer?" the model might give factual info, but it won't catch the fear
or emotional weight behind the question. Saver says it clearly: understanding
the real reason behind a question is critical in healthcare. Right now, Al can't
ask follow-ups or sense what's left unsaid.
Building a full picture of the patient
Doctors don't just listen, they interpret. Based on how a patient responds,
they might realize that back pain is stress-related, posture-related, or
something worse. LLMs don't have that kind of flexibility. They can't adjust
on the fly or connect the dots in the same way.
Handling unclear or incomplete info
Clinicians ask follow-ups to resolve gaps or inconsistencies. LLMs sometimes
fill in the blanks with confident guesses, which can be dangerous.
Hallucinations, made-up studies, or misread clinical details aren't just
inconvenient; they can break trust and put patients at risk if not caught.

Case studies: successful AI implementation in healthcare
Saver highlights that even the best Al model won't succeed without smart implementation. Two real-world examples
show how doing it right makes all the difference.
Predicting sepsis before it's too late
Sepsis is one of the top causes of death in hospitals. Most AI tools
detect it after a patient is already in serious trouble. However,
Vituity built a model that flags risk before symptoms show up, using
trends in lab results.
What's smart is how they introduced it. The risk score is added to
the normal lab report instead of triggering an alarm or making
doctors click through a new dashboard. No extra steps, no new
system to learn.
Doctors don't even realize they're using Al; they just see more useful
lab data. This "invisible Al" setup helped them catch sepsis earlier,
act faster, and save lives. Tamara Saver points out that adoption
was seamless because it blended into existing workflows. No
resistance, just results.
The "Al Resident" that earned doctors' trust
Getting doctors to trust AI is another hurdle, especially if it feels
like a black box or comes off too sure of itself. But there's a clever
workaround: speak their language.
Vituity called its system an "Al Resident", a digital version of a junior
doctor. Instead of saying "Al recommends," the message says, "Your Al
Resident suggests ... pending your review."
That small shift made a big impact. It felt familiar and collaborative,
not bossy or rigid. Clinicians were more open to using it, which led to
better uptake and better outcomes. As Saver explains, AI gains traction
when it acts as backup, not a replacement for human judgment.

Bringing a new drug from concept to market is notoriously slow and expensive. In fact, the
process can often take over a decade and cost billions of dollars, with most attempts still ending
in failure.
Despite the abundance of data generated through clinical trials, the pharmaceutical industry
struggles to systematically capture and analyze these failures, frequently repeating past mistakes.

mindset, Barella argues, hampers innovation, slows progress, and prevents the industry from

Integrating AI-enabled real-time health monitoring and personalized medicine solutions promises
to shift healthcare from reactive to proactive, significantly reducing misdiagnoses and adverse

The implications of this approach extend well beyond pharma. Barella's insights underscore a

As AI-first thinking becomes mainstream, the pharmaceutical industry (and healthcare at large)

The problem: Why pharma moves too slowly
Bringing a new drug to market takes time, often over a decade, and billions of dollars. And still,
most drug candidates don't make it. Pharma is an industry built on failure, yet it rarely learns
from those failures meaningfully and systematically.
One of the biggest roadblocks is poor data infrastructure. Clinical trials produce tons of valuable
information, but much of it ends up siloed, ignored, or lost. When a trial fails, the lessons often
vanish; no insights, improvements, just repeated mistakes.
As Barella puts it, pharma tends to operate like NASA: highly controlled, risk-averse, and slow to
move. In 2023, a single faulty gasket delayed the Artemis mission. In drug development, small
issues often lead to cautious decisions that drag the process out even more.
We still lean heavily on human judgment and one-off diagnostics, like annual checkups or single
lab results. It's a reactive model, and it's slow. Failed trials and rejected drugs often aren't mined
for insights, so we miss the chance to get better.
Barella sums it up:
"We manage science when we should be engineering it."
Until pharma adopts a mindset of fast iteration and continuous learning, it'll keep moving at a
crawl, missing out on the real potential for rapid progress.

The solution: Using AI to learn from failure
Takeda is investing in AI to reshape how pharma handles failure, turning setbacks into smart, data-driven learning moments.
The focus is on using AI to analyze why things go wrong and ensure those lessons drive progress.
What pharma can learn from SpaceX
Take a look at two very different approaches to space exploration: NASA
vs. Spacex.
NASA has a long track record of success, but operates with a zero-failure
mindset. Everything is planned down to the last detail, and mistakes are
avoided. In 2023, a single faulty gasket stopped the Artemis rocket rollout,
proving how risk-averse the culture is.
Spacex, on the other hand, leans into failure. Their rockets blow up during
tests, and that's the point. Every crash is packed with data. Each test,
even the failed ones, moves them forward faster.
Pharma needs to think more like SpaceX. A failed clinical trial or rejected
drug shouldn't be a dead end. It should be a rich source of insight.
With AI, companies can track every part of the process, analyze what
happened, and use that knowledge to make smarter choices next time.
Failure isn't the enemy; it's fuel for faster innovation.
"Pharma must think more like SpaceX: fail fast, learn faster,
and improve continuously."
AI-powered drug discovery
Generative AI is changing how new drugs are discovered. These models
can simulate how molecules interact with diseases, speeding up the
process of identifying promising candidates in the early stages.
AI also helps teams hold onto hard-earned knowledge. Even if a scientist
leaves, the insights they've contributed don't walk out the door; Al
systems keep that institutional memory intact.
And with access to massive datasets, from genomics and metabolomics
to clinical records, AI can spot hidden patterns and connections that
would've been impossible to find manually.
"We are an industry built on failure, but we lack a systematic
way to remember and learn from it. Al can change that."

often means treatments don't work as well as they could. And drug interactions are still one of

AI can also flag potential drug interactions before they become dangerous, giving doctors and

It's even possible to create "batch of one" medications, tailored to a person's specific genetic
and biochemical makeup.
And with connected sensors tracking real-time data, early signs of side effects can be caught
and addressed quickly, before they spiral into something worse.
The result? Less trial-and-error, fewer side effects, and way better outcomes.
"Drug interaction fatalities in the U.S. are equivalent to a

Making AI safe for healthcare: The role of LLMOps
McDonald and his team built a complete LLMOps framework, an evolution of DevOps and MLOps to bring stability to the
unpredictable world of generative AI. Still, they were designed specifically for large language models in clinical settings.
This wasn't a plug-and-play solution. It was built around five specialized pipelines, each designed to handle a different
part of the system:
Development pipeline. Borrowing from traditional software
development, this pipeline applied strict testing and quality checks-not
just to code, but to prompts, data flows, and AI outputs.
V
Infrastructure pipeline. Managed entirely through Infrastructure as
Code (IaC), this ensured cloud environments could be quickly deployed,
version-controlled, and closely monitored.
>
Administration pipeline. Before the AI touched any real patient data, it
was stress-tested in a sandbox. This let the team evaluate outputs and
catch issues early.
Execution pipeline. Once live, the system stayed under close watch.
Human-in-the-loop oversight meant that any strange or risky behavior
could be caught and fixed in real time.
R&D pipeline. This is where the system learned and improved.
Real-world feedback was used to retrain and refine the models, without
ever compromising compliance or performance.
"Large language models are wild and unpredictable. But with the right
